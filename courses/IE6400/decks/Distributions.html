HIS QUESTION

n BERNOULLI TRIALS

Binomial Distribution

Repeat a Bernoulli trial $n$ times. Count the successes.

PMF
$$P(X{=}k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$$
PARAMETERS
$$E[X] = np$$
$$\text{Var}(X) = np(1-p)$$
TRAITS
Fixed $n$ trials
Independent trials
Same $p$ each time
Discrete: 0, 1, ..., n

Steph Curry: 10 Free Throws

Steph Curry has a career free throw success rate of 91%. In tonight's game, he takes 10 free throws. Each shot is independent of the others.

Q1: P(makes all 10)?
Q2: Expected makes?
Q3: P(exactly 8)?
STEP 1 — DEFINE R.V.
Let $X \triangleq$ free throws made (out of 10)
$x \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots, 10\}$
STEP 2 — IDENTIFY DISTRIBUTION
$X \sim \text{Bin}(n{=}10,\; p{=}0.91)$
Why? Fixed n=10, independent shots, same p each time, counting successes.
STEP 3 — COMPUTE
Q1: P(all 10)
$0.91^{10}$
$= \boxed{0.389}$
Q2: E[X]
$np = 10 \times 0.91$
$= \boxed{9.1}$
Q3: P(X=8)
$\binom{10}{8}(0.91)^8(0.09)^2$
$= \boxed{0.172}$
STEP 4 — INTERPRET
Curry has a 39% chance of going 10-for-10. On average: 9.1 out of 10.

Normal Distribution

$$X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2) \qquad f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$$

$$\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$$

$\pm 1\sigma$: 68%
$\pm 2\sigma$: 95%

TRAITS
Symmetric about $\mu$
Continuous: $(-\infty, \infty)$
Mean = median = mode
Thin tails

The Z-Score

The Z-Transform
$$Z = \dfrac{X - \mu}{\sigma} \qquad Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\,1)$$

Convert any normal to the standard normal — then use one table for everything.

2. Look up $P(Z \le z)$ in Z-table
3. That's your CDF value — done
KEY VALUES

$P(Z \le 1) = 0.8413$

THE KEY TRANSFORMATION

From Any Normal → Standard Normal

Every normal is a shifted & stretched version of $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. The Z-score undoes both.

WHAT Z DOES
Subtract μ → center at 0
Divide by σ → scale to 1
WHY STANDARDIZE?
One table for all normals. Compare apples to oranges.
EXAMPLE
$X=380$, $\mu=350$, $\sigma=20$:
$Z = \frac{380-350}{20} = 1.5$

QUESTION

Tesla Battery: Will You Make It?

Tesla Model 3 battery ranges have a mean of 350 miles with a standard deviation of 20 miles. You need to drive 380 miles.

Find: What is the probability your Tesla can make the trip?
STEP 1 — DEFINE R.V.
Let $X \triangleq$ battery range (miles)
$x \in \mathbb{R}$  (effectively $x \geq 0$)
STEP 2 — IDENTIFY DISTRIBUTION
$X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu{=}350,\; \sigma{=}20)$
Why? Range varies naturally — continuous, symmetric, bell-shaped.
STEP 3 — COMPUTE (Z-table lookup)
1.  Standardize:  $Z = \dfrac{380 - 350}{20} = 1.5$
2.  Z-table:  row 1.5, col .00  →  $\Phi(1.5) = 0.9332$
3.  Upper tail:  $P(X > 380) = 1 - 0.9332 = \boxed{0.0668}$
STEP 4 — INTERPRET
Only 6.7% chance of making 380 miles — about 1 in 15. Better charge up!

Machine Calibration: Bolt Diameter

A CNC machine produces bolts with mean diameter 10mm and standard deviation 0.1mm. The spec requires bolts between 9.8mm and 10.2mm.

Q1: P(within spec)?   Q2: Reject rate?   Q3: Rejects per 10,000?
STEP 1 — DEFINE R.V.
Let $X \triangleq$ bolt diameter (mm)
$x \in \mathbb{R}$  (effectively $x \geq 0$)
STEP 2 — IDENTIFY DISTRIBUTION
$X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu{=}10,\; \sigma{=}0.1)$
Why? Machining precision — continuous, symmetric variation around target.
STEP 3 — COMPUTE
$Z_1 = \frac{9.8-10}{0.1} = -2$,   $Z_2 = \frac{10.2-10}{0.1} = 2$  →  $P(-2 \le Z \le 2) = \boxed{95.44\%}$
Reject rate: $1 - 0.9544 = \boxed{4.56\%}$  →  In 10,000 bolts: $\boxed{456}$ rejected
STEP 4 — INTERPRET
95.4% of bolts pass spec. If $\sigma$ improves to 0.05mm → only 6 rejects per 10,000!

How Much Falls Within 1σ, 2σ, 3σ?

For any normal distribution — from heights to manufacturing tolerances — the same percentages apply. This is the “68–95–99.7 rule”.

POISSON PROCESS · KEY PROPERTY

Poisson Scales with Interval

If events arrive at a constant rate, then the expected count is proportional to the interval length. Cut the time in half, halve the rate.

Time-Scaling Rule
$$X_t \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda \cdot t)$$
where $\lambda$ is the rate per unit time and $t$ is the interval length

3
expected calls
$X \sim \text{Poi}(12 \times \tfrac{1}{4})$
$= \text{Poi}(3)$
1 HOUR
12
expected calls
$X \sim \text{Poi}(12 \times 1)$
$= \text{Poi}(12)$
8-HOUR SHIFT

expected calls
$X \sim \text{Poi}(12 \times 8)$
$= \text{Poi}(96)$
⚠ Caveat: The rate $\lambda$ must stay constant across the interval. If lunch hour spikes the rate, you can't just multiply.

Rate $\lambda$

✓ APPROX VALID
When OK?
$\lambda \ge 10$ usually works.
$\lambda \ge 20$ is excellent.

THE FORMULA

Poisson → Normal Approximation

When $\lambda$ is large enough
$$X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda) \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; X \approx Y \sim \mathcal{N}(\lambda,\, \lambda)$$
Same mean. Same variance. Continuous instead of discrete.
① STANDARDIZE
$$Z = \dfrac{k - \lambda}{\sqrt{\lambda}}$$
Convert any value $k$ to a Z-score using $\mu = \lambda$ and $\sigma = \sqrt{\lambda}$.
② CONTINUITY CORRECTION
$$P(X = k) \approx P(k{-}\tfrac{1}{2} \le Y \le k{+}\tfrac{1}{2})$$
Discrete → continuous needs a half-step adjustment for accuracy.
③ WHEN VALID
$\lambda \ge 10$  → usually OK
$\lambda \ge 20$  → excellent
$\lambda < 5$  → don't

NORMAL APPROXIMATION EXAMPLE

Call Center: 100 Calls/Hour

A call center receives an average of 100 calls per hour, arriving independently and at a constant rate. Using the Normal approximation, find $P(X \ge 115)$ — the probability of 115 or more calls in an hour.

Why approximate? Computing $\sum_{k=115}^{\infty} \frac{e^{-100} 100^k}{k!}$ exactly is impractical. With $\lambda{=}100 \gg 10$, the Normal approximation is excellent.
STEP 1 — APPROXIMATE
$X \sim \text{Poisson}(100)$
$\Rightarrow Y \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu{=}100,\, \sigma^2{=}100)$
$\sigma = \sqrt{100} = 10$
STEP 2 — CONTINUITY CORRECTION
$P(X \ge 115) \approx P(Y \ge 114.5)$
(half-step shift since Poisson is discrete)
STEP 3 — STANDARDIZE & LOOK UP
$Z = \dfrac{114.5 - 100}{10} = 1.45$  →  $\Phi(1.45) = 0.9265$
$P(X \ge 115) \approx P(Z \ge 1.45) = 1 - 0.9265 = \boxed{0.0735}$
STEP 4 — INTERPRET
About a 7.4% chance the center sees 115+ calls in an hour. (Exact Poisson answer: 0.0726 — the approximation is within 1%.)
