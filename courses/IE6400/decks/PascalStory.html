The Gambling Problem
That Changed Everything
Paris, 1654
Paris, 1654
A tavern in the heart of Paris.
Two men sit across from each other. Between them: a pile of 100 gold coins, a pair of dice, and a deal.
First to win 3 rounds takes everything.
The score: 2 - 1
Then the game stops.
Player A has won 2 rounds. Player B has won 1. But something forces them to leave — the game can't continue.
"How do we split the 100 coins fairly?"
The Debate
Nobody can agree.
Player A says: "I was winning — give me everything!"
Player B says: "I still had a chance — split it 50/50!"
A wealthy gambler named Chevalier de Mere takes this unsolved puzzle to the smartest man he knows.
The Mathematician
Blaise Pascal picks up his quill.
He's intrigued — but he can't solve it alone. So he writes a letter to the only other person in the world who might understand.
The Correspondence
Pascal writes to Fermat.
Pierre de Fermat — a lawyer by day, mathematician by night. For months, they exchange letters about dice, cards, and fairness. They argue. They calculate. They invent.
The Breakthrough
They find the answer.
Look at every possible way the game could end. Count how many go to each player.
75
coins to Player A
25
coins to Player B
Not 100-0. Not 50-50. The mathematically fair split.
What they invented
Probability theory is born.
From those letters came: insurance, statistics, machine learning, quantum physics, weather forecasting, finance, medicine, AI.
All because a gambler wanted his money.
